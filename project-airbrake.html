Mission Profile

For the 2026 International Rocket Engineering Competition (IREC), the University of Georgia Rocketry Team is targeting a precise apogee of 10,000 ft (3048 m). Passive flight is highly susceptible to atmospheric variance; to guarantee accuracy, we engineered an active airbrake system to modulate drag in real-time.

The design constraints were strict: the mechanism had to fit within a 225 mm axial length inside a 6-inch airframe, weigh under 3.2 kg, cost less than $400, and draw no more than 25 A of current to preserve battery life for avionics.

Mechanical Architecture

The solution is a three-flap system driven by a single NEO 550 BLDC motor through a 4:1 gearbox. The motor rotates a central 1204 ball screw, driving a custom CNC-machined 6061-T6 aluminum cuff linearly. This linear motion is converted into radial flap deployment via a double-linkage system.

Material Selection: We selected 7075-T6 aluminum for the arms and linkages due to its high strength-to-weight ratio and fatigue resistance. The internal ribs and bulkheads are waterjet-cut from G10 fiberglass, ensuring alignment tolerance within ±0.5 mm while maintaining radio transparency for internal avionics.

Analysis & Optimization

Kinematics: We developed a custom kinematic-force optimization model in Python to minimize current draw. By iterating through thousands of linkage geometries, we optimized the torque curve to ensure the motor operates efficiently even under worst-case aerodynamic loads (Mach 0.981).

Angle: 0.0°

CFD & FEA: Computational Fluid Dynamics (Ansys Fluent) at Mach 0.7 and 80° deployment verified a total drag force of 320 N. Structural Finite Element Analysis confirmed that critical stress points in the linkage pins remained well below yield strength (FS > 2.25).

Control Strategy

The system is controlled by an STM32H5 microcontroller. We validated control algorithms using RocketPy, running over 370,000 Monte Carlo flight simulations.

While PID and LQR controllers were tested, an Explicit Model Predictive Controller (EMPC) yielded the lowest Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) for apogee targeting, demonstrating superior robustness against wind gusts and motor performance variance.
